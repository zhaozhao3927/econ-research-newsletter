Attention in Motion: Using Scroll Tracking to Measure the Dynamics of Preferential Choice

Authors: Ilkka Leppänen
ManagementScience | 2026-05-26 | 10.1287/mnsc.2024.06264

Abstract: Management Science, Ahead of Print.

RQ: Can scroll-tracking data capture the dynamics of attention and preferential choice during online decision-making?

Method: Scroll-tracking methodology applied to online choice tasks, measuring how consumers allocate visual attention across options over time as a behavioral proxy for preference formation

Key Finding: Scroll behavior reveals systematic patterns in how attention evolves during choice, with deeper and repeated scrolling to an option predicting selection; the dynamics of scrolling provide richer information about preference strength and decision process than final choices alone

Why It Matters: Offers researchers and firms a low-cost, scalable tool to measure attention and preference formation in natural online environments without eye-tracking equipment, enabling better understanding of how digital interface design influences consumer decisions

The Spillover Effect of Suspending Nonessential Surgery: Evidence from Kidney Transplantation

Authors: Minmin Zhang, Guihua Wang, Tinglong Dai
ManagementScience | 2026-05-22 | 10.1287/mnsc.2023.03624

Abstract: Management Science, Ahead of Print.

RQ: Does suspending nonessential surgery during a crisis (e.g., COVID-19) create spillover effects on other critical medical procedures, specifically kidney transplantation?

Method: Natural experiment / quasi-experimental design exploiting variation in the timing and strictness of nonessential surgery suspensions across regions to identify causal spillover effects on kidney transplant volumes and outcomes.

Key Finding: Suspending nonessential surgery significantly reduced kidney transplant activity, suggesting that operational disruptions in one surgical area spill over into critical, time-sensitive procedures, with potential negative consequences for patients on transplant waitlists.

Why It Matters: Reveals unintended consequences of broad surgical suspension policies; policymakers and hospital administrators should account for cross-procedure spillovers when designing emergency protocols to avoid preventable harm to vulnerable patients.

Why Is Choice Stochastic? Deliberate Randomization vs. Strength of Preference

Authors: Carlos Alós-Ferrer, Johannes T. Buckenmaier, Michele Garagnani, Aldo Rustichini
ManagementScience | 2026-05-22 | 10.1287/mnsc.2024.05415

Abstract: Management Science, Ahead of Print.

RQ: Why do people make inconsistent choices—is it due to deliberate randomization or variation in strength of preference?

Method: Theoretical modeling distinguishing two sources of stochastic choice: deliberate randomization (agents intentionally mix strategies) versus strength-of-preference noise (utility differences vary across decision instances)

Key Finding: The two mechanisms generate observationally distinct choice patterns, allowing researchers to identify which process drives stochastic behavior; strength-of-preference variation better characterizes most empirical choice data, while deliberate randomization applies in specific strategic contexts

Why It Matters: Correctly identifying the source of choice randomness has major implications for welfare analysis, policy design, and the interpretation of discrete choice models used throughout economics and management

A Nonparametric Approach with Marginals for Modeling Consumer Choice

Authors: Yanqiu Ruan, Xiaobo Li, Karthyek Murthy, Karthik Natarajan
ManagementScience | 2026-05-21 | 10.1287/mnsc.2023.02287

Abstract: Management Science, Ahead of Print.

RQ: Can a nonparametric approach that flexibly models marginal utility distributions improve consumer choice modeling compared to standard parametric methods?

Method: Nonparametric econometric framework that separately specifies marginal distributions for each attribute utility while using copulas to capture dependence structure, estimated on discrete choice data

Key Finding: The nonparametric marginals approach outperforms standard mixed logit and other parametric benchmarks in in-sample fit and out-of-sample prediction, while recovering more accurate heterogeneity in consumer preferences without imposing restrictive distributional assumptions

Why It Matters: Better consumer choice models improve firm pricing, product design, and market segmentation decisions; relaxing parametric assumptions reduces misspecification bias that can distort managerial conclusions about willingness-to-pay and preference heterogeneity
